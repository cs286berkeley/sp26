Course Description

The data systems field, with its roots in database systems, has been exploring issues in data storage, management, processing, and analysis for over 50 years. Over this time, it has evolved from supporting business record-keeping to becoming a foundational pillar of modern computing. Data systems today underpin nearly every end-user application, enable insight discovery over massive datasets, and have given rise to several multi-billion-dollar industries. In our data-rich 21st century, the lessons of the field apply increasingly broadly across computing and beyond.

Indeed, the data systems field—by virtue of its focus on humans and applications interacting with data at scale—represents a natural cross-section of computer science. The field spans and integrates ideas from systems, theory, programming languages, computer architecture, and human–computer interaction, among others. As such, it serves as a unifying lens through which many fundamental trade-offs in computing can be understood.

In this course we will study a range of papers from both the distant and recent past. Given the breadth of the field, it is not possible to cover it comprehensively. Our focus will largely be on core topics in data systems that are still relevant today. While the emphasis will be on more systems-oriented perspectives, we will not shy away from theoretical results where relevant. The readings will mix classic papers that have stood the test of time, alongside emerging new topics, and revisit topics that may be ripe for revival given current technology trends.

Prerequisites

The class is open to graduate students, as well as advanced undergraduates. If you're an undergraduate and would like to enroll, please fill out this form here. I am happy to consider undergraduates who have adequate background, especially with CS 186 (and possibly Data 101) and CS 162. Experience taking a graduate research class such as CS 262 or experience working on research in data systems/hands-on development with data systems is a plus.

If this is your first exposure to a graduate paper-reading-oriented class, here are some useful references: How to Read a Paper and Writing Reviews for Systems Conferences.

Course Format

This is a graduate seminar, and student participation is a key part of the experience. The course will be held in-person, and regular attendance is mandatory.

The bulk of the course will involve learning from research papers, both classics and recent results. Students will be expected to read papers in advance of class, and participate in class discussion. Students will also undertake independent research as part of the course.

Grading breakdown will be as follows:

  • Project: 75%
  • Weekly Summaries: 10%. Fill in using a Google form before class, lightly graded; read all assigned papers per class, and pick one for which you share something interesting you took away (i.e., a short para, even 3-4 lines OK!)—not an LLM-generated summary. Skip any four classes.
  • Class Participation: 15%. Attending and asking and answering questions, comments, feedback, etc. Depending on class size, class participation could possibly include an in-class presentation of a research paper.
